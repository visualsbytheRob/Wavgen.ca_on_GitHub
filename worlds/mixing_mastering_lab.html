Compressor Type Laboratory
Four compression characters — same source, different response curves
A/B COMPARE:

Circuit topology shapes character: VCA (voltage-controlled amplifier) responds in microseconds — ideal for bus glue. FET (field-effect transistor) adds harmonic edge and punch. Optical compressors react to light intensity — inherently non-linear, musical. Variable-Mu uses tube gain reduction — the slowest and most gentle, for "gluing" a mix.

Stereo Field Visualizer
Phase scope (Lissajous) — visualizing stereo width and mono compatibility

Reading the phase scope: Vertical line = perfect mono. Oval tilted 45° = healthy stereo. Nearly horizontal = over-wide (mono-incompatible). Red areas = out-of-phase content that will cancel in mono.

Parallel Compression Builder
New York compression — blend dynamics with density

Parallel compression preserves the natural transients and dynamics of the dry signal while adding the density and sustained body of a heavily compressed version underneath. At 40% wet blend, you get the best of both: punch from the dry, sustain from the wet. The "New York" technique uses infinite ratio on the wet path.

Vinyl Mastering Lab
The physical constraints of the groove — a different master entirely

Vinyl is not digital. The physical groove has hard limits. Bass below ~150Hz must be mono — a wide stereo bass causes the stylus to skip. Sibilance cuts too deep and damages the groove wall. The cutter head has maximum excursion limits that translate directly to maximum level. A vinyl master requires different processing than a streaming master.

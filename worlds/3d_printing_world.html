3D Printing

Additive manufacturing — layer by layer, complexity becomes free

Topology Optimization

Topology optimization removes material from regions that carry no structural load, producing forms impossible with subtractive manufacturing. The algorithm distributes material only where stress flows, creating organic skeletal structures that are simultaneously lighter and stronger than solid equivalents.

SIMP Method

Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization. Iteratively redistributes material density. Standard algorithm in Altair OptiStruct, nTopology, and Fusion 360.

Lattice Infill

Topology optimization at the micro-scale: gyroid, Schwartz P, octet truss, and Kelvin cell infill structures replace solid material with graded lattices.

Multi-Load Cases

Real parts experience many loading conditions. Optimization over multiple simultaneous or alternating loads produces more robust structures.

FEA Validation

Finite Element Analysis validates optimized designs before print. Stress concentration, deformation, and safety factor verified computationally.

The Slicing Process

Slicer software converts 3D geometry (STL, 3MF, OBJ) into layer-by-layer toolpath instructions (G-code). Every slicing parameter — layer height, infill pattern, support strategy, perimeter count — affects structural properties, surface quality, print time, and material use.

Layer Height

0.1–0.3mm for FDM. Thinner = smoother surface, longer print. Layer height is the primary quality-speed trade-off lever in FDM printing.

Infill Patterns

Grid, gyroid, honeycomb, triangles, cubic, lightning — each pattern has different isotropy, strength-per-gram, and print speed characteristics.

Support Structures

Auto-generated supports for overhangs >45°. Tree supports (Chitubox, Bambu, Cura) minimize contact area and material use.

Perimeters / Walls

Outer perimeters determine surface quality and part strength. 2–4 perimeters typical; structural parts may use 6+ or 100% infill perimeters.

Printing Technologies

Additive manufacturing encompasses radically different physical processes. Each technology trades resolution, material range, build volume, surface quality, and cost differently. The right technology depends on the application, not just the available budget.

technology comparison — click to explore

FDM

Fused Deposition Modeling. Thermoplastic filament extruded through heated nozzle. Most accessible: Bambu Lab, Prusa, Creality. Layer height 0.1–0.3mm.

SLA / MSLA

Stereolithography. UV-cured photopolymer resin. Sub-0.05mm detail, smooth surfaces. Formlabs, Elegoo, Anycubic. Brittle without post-cure.

SLS

Selective Laser Sintering. Nylon powder fused by laser. No support structures needed. Best for functional assemblies. Industrial: EOS, Fuse 1 (Formlabs).

DMLS / SLM

Direct Metal Laser Sintering. Metal powder fused by high-power laser. Titanium, Inconel, stainless steel. Aerospace, medical, tooling applications.

Binder Jetting

Liquid binder deposited onto powder bed. High-speed multi-material. HP Multi Jet Fusion, ExOne. Used for sand casting molds and full-color parts.

Material Jetting

Photopolymer droplets deposited and UV-cured. Stratasys PolyJet, 3D Systems ProJet. Multi-material, multi-color in one print. Dental, product design.

Material Science

Material choice determines everything: mechanical properties, thermal performance, chemical resistance, and biocompatibility. The range of printable materials has expanded from basic PLA to engineering thermoplastics, composites, ceramics, bio-inks, and multi-material gradient structures.

material property radar
PLA

PLA

Polylactic acid. Biodegradable, easy to print, brittle. Ideal for prototypes, art, and non-structural parts. Biodegrades in industrial composters.

PETG

Balance of strength, flexibility, and ease of printing. Chemical resistance. Food-safe variants. Preferred for functional prototypes and enclosures.

TPU / TPE

Flexible thermoplastics. Shore hardness 60A–95A. Gaskets, grips, wearables, shock absorbers. Stretchable up to 600% elongation at break.

Carbon Fiber Composites

Chopped CF in Nylon or PLA matrix: 2–5× stiffness of base polymer. Continuous CF (Markforged): aerospace-grade strength-to-weight ratio.

Post-Processing

The print is rarely the final product. Post-processing transforms rough layer-line surfaces into finished parts: support removal, surface treatment, heat treatment, plating, painting, and functional finishing. For SLA and resin prints, UV post-cure is mandatory for full material properties.

Raw Print

Sanding & Priming

Wet sanding from 80–2000 grit removes layer lines. Polyurethane primer fills micro-texture. Achieves surface roughness of Ra 0.1–0.5μm.

Vapor Smoothing

Acetone vapor (ABS) or Polymaker Polysher (ASA/PLA) reflow the surface thermally. Requires ventilation. Achieves injection-molded appearance in minutes.

Electroplating

Conductive paint + electroplating deposits copper, nickel, or chrome on plastic substrates. Used for decorative metal finish and EMI shielding.

HIP (Hot Isostatic Press)

Applied to metal SLS/DMLS parts. Eliminates internal voids via isostatic pressure at elevated temperature. Required for aerospace fatigue life compliance.

Design for Additive Manufacturing

DfAM inverts the design constraints of subtractive manufacturing. Complexity is nearly free — undercuts, internal channels, overhangs, and organic geometry impose no machining cost penalty. But new constraints emerge: anisotropy, support accessibility, and layer bonding must be designed for, not against.

DfAM feature explorer

Internal Channels

Conformal cooling channels in injection molds match the mold surface geometry — impossible with drilled channels, trivial with DMLS. Reduces cycle time 20–40%.

Overhang Rules

FDM overhang limit: 45° from vertical without supports. SLA: 60–70°. SLS: no limit. Orient parts to minimize supports and maximize surface quality on critical faces.

Minimum Wall

FDM: 0.8mm (2× nozzle diameter). SLA: 0.4mm. DMLS: 0.3mm. Thin walls risk warping, delamination, or print failure — check with slicer preview.

Part Consolidation

Replace assemblies of 10+ machined parts with 1–2 printed parts. Reduces fasteners, alignment fixtures, and assembly labor. GE's LEAP fuel nozzle: 20 parts → 1.

Glossary

Core vocabulary of additive manufacturing, material science, and design for AM.

FDM
Fused Deposition Modeling. Extrudes thermoplastic filament layer by layer. Most accessible AM process. Also called FFF (Fused Filament Fabrication).
SLA
Stereolithography Apparatus. UV laser or masked LCD cures liquid photopolymer resin layer by layer. Highest detail among consumer processes.
SLS
Selective Laser Sintering. CO₂ laser fuses powder (nylon, glass-filled, rubber). No support structures. Best functional part quality without post-process finishing.
DMLS
Direct Metal Laser Sintering. High-power fiber laser melts metal powder. Titanium, Inconel, steel, aluminum. Used in aerospace and medical implants.
G-Code
Machine instruction language for CNC and 3D printers. Specifies position (X,Y,Z), extrusion (E), temperature (M104/M109), fan speed, and movement type.
Topology Optimization
Computational method removing material from low-stress regions while maintaining structural performance. Produces organic, biomorphic forms.
Infill
Internal structure density and pattern. 0% = hollow shell; 100% = solid. Typical functional parts: 15–40%. Pattern affects anisotropy and strength direction.
Gyroid
Triply periodic minimal surface (TPMS). Produces isotropic infill — equal strength in all directions. Also used as scaffold in tissue engineering.
Support Material
Sacrificial printed structure that supports overhangs during printing. Removed mechanically or dissolved (PVA in water, HIPS in limonene).
Warping
Differential thermal contraction causing print corners to lift from bed. Mitigated by heated enclosures, bed adhesion (PEI, glue, ABS slurry), and brim printing.
Layer Adhesion
Bond strength between deposited layers. Determines Z-axis tensile strength. Typically 20–50% of XY strength in FDM. Improved by higher temperature and slower speed.
Anisotropy
Direction-dependent mechanical properties. FDM parts are weakest in Z (layer separation). SLS parts approach isotropy. Design must account for print orientation.
DfAM
Design for Additive Manufacturing. Design philosophy exploiting AM capabilities: internal channels, organic geometry, part consolidation, and gradient material.
Post-Cure
UV exposure after SLA/MSLA printing that completes resin polymerization. Required for full mechanical properties and reduces tackiness and brittleness.
Part Consolidation
Replacing multi-component assemblies with fewer printed parts. GE LEAP nozzle: 20 parts → 1. Eliminates fasteners, welds, and alignment fixtures.
Voxel
3D pixel — the fundamental unit of some AM processes (Material Jetting). Enables gradient material, color, and property control at the voxel level.
Build Volume
Maximum printable geometry envelope (X×Y×Z). Consumer FDM: 220–350mm. Large-format industrial: 500–1000mm. Pellet extrusion: meters scale.
Overhang Angle
Angle from vertical that self-supporting geometry can achieve. FDM: ~45°. SLA: ~60°. Beyond limit, support structures required or print quality degrades.
Nozzle Diameter
Determines minimum feature size and maximum extrusion rate. 0.4mm standard; 0.25mm fine detail; 0.8–1.0mm fast structural printing.
Generative Design
AI/optimization-driven form-finding using performance constraints as inputs. Autodesk Fusion, nTopology. Explores thousands of valid topologies computationally.
3MF
3D Manufacturing Format. Open XML-based format replacing STL. Preserves color, materials, metadata, and lattice data. Industry standard for modern slicers.
WAAM
Wire Arc Additive Manufacturing. MIG/TIG welding robot deposits metal beads. Large-scale metal AM (meters). MX3D used it to print an Amsterdam canal bridge.
